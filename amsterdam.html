Amsterdam

Jul 20–23  ·  4 nights Rosewood Amsterdam  ·  Prinsengracht 432–436
Museums
Rijksmuseum
The national museum of the Netherlands. The undisputed centerpiece is Rembrandt's Night Watch — the largest painting in the building and one of the greatest in the world. Also houses Vermeer's The Milkmaid and Woman Reading a Letter. Book tickets in advance; queues are long without them.
Van Gogh Museum
The largest collection of Van Gogh's work in the world — over 200 paintings and 500 drawings, displayed chronologically so you follow his arc from the dark Dutch period through the explosion of color in Arles and Saint-Rémy. Book in advance — this sells out weeks ahead in summer.
Anne Frank House
The Achterhuis — the secret annex where Anne Frank, her family, and four others hid from the Nazis for over two years. One of the most affecting places in Europe. Tickets must be booked online well in advance — walk-ins are nearly impossible. The house is left almost bare by design: no furniture, just the diary entries and the outline of the space.
Landmarks & City Center
Amsterdam Canals
The canal ring — Herengracht, Keizersgracht, Prinsengracht — is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, built in the Dutch Golden Age of the 17th century. Amsterdam has 165 canals and over 1,200 bridges. The best orientation is to walk the ring roads or take a boat.
Dam Square & Royal Palace
Dam Square is the historic heart of the city — built on the original dam across the Amstel River, giving Amsterdam its name. The Royal Palace on the square was built in 1648 as the city hall, the largest secular building in the Dutch Golden Age. It's open to visitors; the interior is striking.
Bloemenmarkt (Flower Market)
The world's only floating flower market, on the Singel canal. The stalls are actually set on barges. Go for the spectacle of it — tulip bulbs, Dutch cheese, and an absurd density of tourists. Best in the morning.
A'dam Lookout
Rooftop observation deck directly across the IJ from Centraal Station — free ferry takes 2 minutes. The view over Amsterdam's canal ring and the harbor is excellent. There's a swing that hangs over the edge if you're into that.
Neighborhoods
Jordaan
The most charming neighborhood in Amsterdam — a former working-class district, now full of independent galleries, small cafés, and brown cafes (bruine kroegen). Walk the narrow streets between Prinsengracht and Westerstraat. The Anne Frank House is on Prinsengracht at the Jordaan's edge.
De Pijp
Amsterdam's most multicultural neighborhood, south of the canal ring. Dense with restaurants, bars, and the Albert Cuyp Market running down its spine. The neighborhood feels more local than the tourist center — good place to eat and wander in the evening.
Red Light District (De Wallen)
Amsterdam's oldest neighborhood, east of Dam Square — medieval in layout, with canals, old churches, and the famous windows. Worth walking through for the history and the surreal atmosphere, especially during the day when it's less hectic. The Oude Kerk (Old Church, 1213) sits at its center.
Parks & Markets
Vondelpark
Amsterdam's main park — 47 hectares, just southwest of the museum district. In summer the open-air theater (Openluchttheater) runs free concerts daily. A good place to decompress between museums; the Rijksmuseum and Van Gogh Museum are a 5-minute walk.
Albert Cuyp Market
Amsterdam's largest street market, running the full length of Albert Cuypstraat in De Pijp. Open daily except Sunday. Dutch street food — stroopwafels hot off the iron, raw herring (haring), poffertjes (mini pancakes). Also produce, cheese, clothing, and everything else.
Keukenhof
The world's largest flower garden — 32 hectares in Lisse, 35 km from Amsterdam. ⚠ Open mid-March to mid-May only. You'll be in Amsterdam July 20–23 — it will be closed. Skip or save for another year.
Activities
Canal Cruise
The best way to understand the canal ring is from water level. Boats depart from near Centraal Station and along the Damrak. A 1-hour loop covers the main ring canals and the harbor. Go in the evening for golden-hour light on the canal houses.
Kalverstraat & Rokin
Amsterdam's main pedestrian shopping strip — Zara, UNIQLO, Weekday, H&M and most high-street brands are here. Rokin runs parallel with more standalone stores. Busy but centrally located between Dam Square and Muntplein.
PC Hooftstraat
Amsterdam's luxury high street, one block from the Rijksmuseum. Hermès, Louis Vuitton, Gucci, Chanel, Bottega Veneta — the full lineup. Short street, walkable in 10 minutes, and the architecture of the canal houses they're housed in is worth seeing regardless.
Magna Plaza
A shopping mall inside a neo-Gothic former post office (1899), directly behind the Royal Palace on Dam Square. Worth going in just for the building — soaring arched interior, ornate ironwork. Mid-range brands inside, but the architecture is the reason to visit.
De Bijenkorf
Amsterdam's premier department store, on Dam Square. Six floors — beauty, fashion, homewares, a good food hall on the top floor. The best single-stop option if you want range without walking the whole city.
Nine Streets (De 9 Straatjes)
Nine short streets crossing the main canals in the Jordaan — independent boutiques, vintage shops, specialty stores. More interesting than Kalverstraat; better for browsing than buying.
Johan Cruijff Arena
Home of Ajax, in Amsterdam Zuidoost — about 20 minutes by metro from the center. Named after Johan Cruijff, the greatest Dutch footballer and one of the most influential players in football history. No match in July, but stadium tours run daily.
